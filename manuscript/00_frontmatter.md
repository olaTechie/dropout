# Reinforcement-Learning-Guided Sequential Intervention to Reduce DTP1-to-DTP3 Vaccine Dropout in Nigeria: A Prediction, Policy Optimisation, and Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

## Abstract

Nigeria carries one of the largest burdens of diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis (DTP) vaccine dropout globally, with children who initiate but fail to complete the three-dose series representing a distinct and preventable immunisation failure [@ref3_adedokun2023zero; @ref4_who2020ia2030; @ref7_adeyinka2025widening]. Using the 2024 Nigeria Demographic and Health Survey (NDHS) — a nationally representative sample of 3,194 DTP1-initiating children aged 12–23 months — we developed cascade-specific XGBoost dropout prediction models for the DTP1-to-DTP2 (T1) and DTP2-to-DTP3 (T2) transitions, an offline reinforcement learning (RL) policy for sequential intervention allocation, and a CHEERS 2022-compliant microsimulation with probabilistic sensitivity analysis [@ref17_collins2024tripod; @ref18_npc2025ndhs]. The national weighted DTP1-to-DTP3 dropout rate was 14.6% (range by zone: 8.6%–19.6%). After post-hoc isotonic recalibration, prediction discriminated excellently at both transitions (T1: AUROC 0.910, 95% CI 0.885–0.932; T2: AUROC 0.943, 95% CI 0.929–0.957). The RL-optimised scenario (S4) increased simulated DTP3 coverage by 1.07 percentage points over the status quo, at an incremental cost of ₦748 per child and an incremental cost-effectiveness ratio of approximately ₦699 per percentage-point coverage gain. These findings demonstrate that data-driven, risk-stratified intervention allocation is feasible within existing Nigerian immunisation budgets and can meaningfully narrow equity gaps in vaccine completion.

## Keywords

DTP vaccination, immunisation dropout, Nigeria, reinforcement learning, microsimulation, cost-effectiveness, health equity

## 1. Introduction

Diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis (DTP) vaccination is among the most cost-effective public-health interventions available, yet global progress toward universal coverage remains uneven [@ref4_who2020ia2030]. The WHO–UNICEF Immunisation Agenda 2030 set a target of 90% DTP3 coverage by 2030, with no country falling below 80% [@ref4_who2020ia2030; @ref50_gavi2022zerodose]. Despite a sustained decline in zero-dose children globally, sub-Saharan Africa — and Nigeria in particular — continues to account for a disproportionate share of the world's unimmunised and under-immunised children [@ref3_adedokun2023zero; @ref7_adeyinka2025widening]. Nigeria hosts the largest absolute number of zero-dose children of any country, and persistent geopolitical heterogeneity means that national headline figures obscure profound subnational inequalities: in 2024, DTP3 coverage in the North Central zone was approximately 80%, compared with more than 91% in the South South [@ref7_adeyinka2025widening; @ref18_npc2025ndhs]. These gaps reflect a complex intersection of socioeconomic deprivation, geographic inaccessibility, and structural health-system constraints that aggregate analyses cannot adequately capture [@ref1_uthman2024systematic; @ref5_tessema2024pentavalent].

A critical conceptual distinction frequently obscured in the immunisation literature is that between *access failure* — never receiving DTP1 — and *dropout* — receiving DTP1 but failing to complete the three-dose series [@ref3_adedokun2023zero; @ref2_sridhar2014missed]. Dropout is a distinct phenomenon governed by different determinants and requiring targeted, sequential responses: a child who has already initiated vaccination has overcome the most substantial access barrier, yet may still fail to return for subsequent doses due to caregiving constraints, supply-side disruptions, or accumulating inter-dose delays [@ref5_tessema2024pentavalent; @ref45_laryea2014determinants]. Prior analyses of Nigerian DHS data document a national DTP1-to-DTP3 dropout rate of 28% in 2018 — down from 46% in 2003 — but with persistently higher rates in rural northern zones [@ref3_adedokun2023zero; @ref2_sridhar2014missed]. At the community level, pentavalent dropout across sub-Saharan Africa averages 20.9%, rising to 46% in western sub-Saharan Africa, with maternal age, home delivery, and household poverty identified as consistent predictors [@ref5_tessema2024pentavalent; @ref43_wiysonge2012individual; @ref42_adegboye2021factors]. Yet most existing studies are descriptive or cross-sectional, and few model the *cascade* as a sequential decision problem amenable to individually tailored intervention.

Despite growing interest in prediction modelling for vaccination outcomes, several critical evidence gaps remain. First, no prior study has applied transition-specific machine-learning prediction separately to the DTP1-to-DTP2 and DTP2-to-DTP3 dropout steps within a TRIPOD-AI framework [@ref17_collins2024tripod], leaving the differential drivers of each transition poorly characterised. Second, while mHealth reminders, community health worker (CHW) outreach, and conditional incentives each have established efficacy [@ref9_eze2021mhealth; @ref49_cochrane2018reminder; @ref11_banerjee2010improving], no study has formalised the sequential intervention allocation problem as a Markov decision process (MDP) and solved it using offline reinforcement learning — an approach that learns risk-stratified policies from observational data without requiring new experimental trials [@ref12_ernst2005treebased; @ref13_kumar2020conservative]. Third, economic evaluations of dropout-reduction strategies in Nigeria have not been conducted with the probabilistic sensitivity analysis and equity analysis required under CHEERS 2022, meaning policymakers lack rigorous evidence to guide National Primary Health Care Development Agency (NPHCDA) budget allocation [@ref21_nphcda2018nsipss].

This study addresses these gaps through three integrated contributions. First, we develop and validate cascade-specific XGBoost dropout prediction models for the T1 (DTP1→DTP2) and DTP2→DTP3 (T2) transitions using the 2024 NDHS, following TRIPOD-AI reporting standards and Andersen's behavioural model for feature organisation [@ref17_collins2024tripod; @ref8_andersen1995revisiting; @ref22_chen2016xgboost]. Second, we formulate the dropout prevention problem as an MDP and train offline RL policies — Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI) and Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) — alongside a contextual bandit for static allocation, reporting policy evaluation under the Gottesman 2019 RL-in-health framework [@ref12_ernst2005treebased; @ref13_kumar2020conservative; @ref14_komorowski2018artificial]. Third, we embed the optimised policy within a CHEERS 2022-compliant individual-level microsimulation incorporating probabilistic sensitivity analysis, equity-weighted cost-effectiveness analysis by wealth quintile, and scenario analysis across five intervention strategies under realistic NPHCDA budget constraints [@ref21_nphcda2018nsipss; @ref38_marseille2015thresholds]. Together, these contributions provide the first integrated predict-optimise-simulate pipeline for DTP dropout reduction in Nigeria, with direct policy implications for the NPHCDA's Reaching Every Ward strategy and the Immunisation Agenda 2030 [@ref4_who2020ia2030; @ref41_who2019digital].
