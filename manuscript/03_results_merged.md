# Results (Merged Opus + Sonnet)

## Study population and immunisation cascade

The analytic sample comprised 3,194 children aged 12–23 months who had received DTP1 in the Nigeria Demographic and Health Survey 2024. Eligibility required the child to be alive at interview, the youngest child per woman, and to have a confirmed DTP1 record (h3 ∈ {1, 2, 3}). The survey-weighted overall DTP1-to-DTP3 dropout rate was 14.8%. Transition-level analysis identified two discrete attrition steps: T1 dropout (DTP1→DTP2 non-completion) occurred in 4.0% of DTP1 recipients (n ≈ 128), and T2 dropout (DTP2→DTP3 non-completion) occurred in 3.9% of DTP2 recipients (n ≈ 119, from a denominator of 3,023 DTP2 recipients). The majority of DTP2 non-recipients (171 children) were excluded from the T2 model.

Zonal cascade analysis revealed substantial geographic heterogeneity (Figure 2). The North Central zone had the highest WHO dropout rate (19.6%; DTP2 retention 92.5%, DTP3 retention 80.4%), followed by the North East (16.8%; DTP3 83.2%) and South East (14.1%; DTP3 85.9%). The South South zone had the lowest dropout (8.6%; DTP3 91.4%). Within-zone T2 retention — the proportion of DTP2 recipients completing DTP3 — was lowest in North Central (86.9%) and highest in South South (94.5%), indicating that late-stage dropout compounds early attrition particularly in northern zones. DTP2 retention exceeded 92% in all zones, confirming that the cascade bottleneck lies primarily at the DTP2-to-DTP3 transition.

## Descriptive characteristics

Table 1 presents the baseline characteristics of the study population stratified by DTP3 completion status. Children who dropped out differed from completers across multiple Andersen domains, including wealth quintile, maternal education, distance to health facility, and prior healthcare contact. Among dynamic temporal features, dropout children had longer inter-dose intervals, greater delay accumulation, and resided in communities with higher cluster-level dropout rates. ## Spatial variation in dropout

The choropleth map of state-level dropout prevalence (Figure 3A) showed widespread geographical variation without a clear north-south gradient, distinguishing dropout from raw DTP3 coverage, which clusters strongly in the northern zones. The funnel plot (Figure 4) identified several states with dropout rates falling outside the 95% control limits, indicating non-random geographic variation beyond sampling error. The local indicators of spatial association (LISA) analysis (Figure 3B) identified isolated spatial outliers but no large contiguous hot-spot or cold-spot clusters, suggesting that dropout is a geographically diffuse phenomenon occurring wherever children enter the immunisation cascade rather than concentrated in specific regions. ## Prediction model discrimination

Transition-specific XGBoost models were trained with Optuna-guided Bayesian hyperparameter optimisation (200 trials per model) using cluster-robust five-fold cross-validation with primary sampling unit blocking (Table 2; Figure 6A-B). Both models substantially outperformed logistic regression. The T1 model (DTP1→DTP2 dropout; n = 3,194; event prevalence 4.0%) achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.910 (95% CI: 0.885–0.932) versus 0.818 for logistic regression (DeLong difference 0.091, z = 6.14, p = 8.1 × 10⁻¹⁰). The T2 model (DTP2→DTP3 dropout; n = 3,023; event prevalence 3.9%) achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.943 (95% CI: 0.929–0.957) versus 0.863 for logistic regression (DeLong difference 0.086, z = 6.11, p = 9.7 × 10⁻¹⁰).

Given the low event prevalence at both transitions (approximately 4%), the area under the precision-recall curve provided a more informative assessment of predictive performance on the minority class: T1 AUC-PR 0.299 (95% CI: 0.230–0.387) and T2 AUC-PR 0.548 (95% CI: 0.454–0.640). Cross-validated fold AUC-ROC values were stable (T1 range: 0.885–0.935; T2 range: 0.924–0.969), confirming the absence of overfitting.

## Prediction model calibration

Raw XGBoost predictions exhibited miscalibration requiring correction prior to downstream reinforcement learning use (Figure 6C-D). The T1 model showed modest under-dispersion (calibration slope 0.874; Brier score 0.036), while the T2 model showed substantially overconfident high-risk predictions (calibration slope 1.596; Brier score 0.027). Isotonic regression recalibration, fit on out-of-fold predictions from the cluster-robust cross-validation, corrected both models: T1 recalibrated slope 0.959 (Brier 0.032; reliability ≈ 0), T2 recalibrated slope 0.969 (Brier 0.026; reliability ≈ 0). All downstream RL trajectory construction and microsimulation inputs used recalibrated model outputs (Supplementary Figure S12).

## SHAP feature importance and Andersen domain decomposition

The Andersen-stratified SHAP decomposition revealed that dynamic temporal features dominated prediction at all three cascade transitions, contrary to the a priori hypothesis (Table 3; Figure 5). For Model T1, the mean absolute SHAP contribution by domain was: dynamic 1.635, need 1.237, enabling 0.897, and predisposing 0.788. Dynamic features thus accounted for 35.9% of total importance (1.635 / 4.557). For Model T2, dynamic dominance was even more pronounced: dynamic 1.240, need 0.414, enabling 0.235, predisposing 0.212, corresponding to 59.0% of total importance (1.240 / 2.101). For the full dropout model, dynamic features accounted for 52.1% (2.739 / 5.256).

The original hypothesis predicted that T1 dropout (early exit) would be dominated by predisposing factors (health beliefs, trust, education) and T2 dropout (late exit) by enabling and need factors (access, cost, competing morbidity). Instead, the leading predictors across all models were temporal cascade features: inter-dose interval, cumulative delay accumulation, community-level dropout rate, and cluster-level DTP coverage — features reflecting the child's vaccination trajectory rather than fixed sociodemographic characteristics. The strongest policy signals for dropout are not who a child is, but how their vaccination history has unfolded. SHAP beeswarm plots for individual features are presented in Supplementary Figure S5.

## Offline reinforcement learning

The MDP was constructed from 6,217 trajectory rows covering 3,138 children across up to two dose-step transitions, with a 55-dimensional state space and five discrete actions. The inferred behaviour policy was heavily SMS-dominated: a₁ (SMS) 70.6%, a₀ (no intervention) 20.9%, a₂ (CHW) 8.3%, a₃ (facility recall) 0.2%, a₄ (incentive) 0.0%.

Fitted Q-iteration with ExtraTrees (200 estimators) converged at iteration 2 (ΔQ = 0.000). Conservative Q-learning was applied with α ∈ {1.0, 2.0, 5.0}; the target of out-of-distribution frequency below 15% was not achievable at any tested α because actions a₃ and a₄ were essentially absent from the data (0.2% and 0.0% respectively), making any policy recommending these actions out-of-distribution by construction. The most conservative setting (α = 5.0) was selected, yielding an OOD frequency of 33.3% (Table 4; Figure 7).

The CQL-learned policy substantially diversified intervention recommendations relative to the behaviour policy (Figure 7A): a₂ (CHW) 29.4%, a₄ (incentive) 22.9%, a₀ (no intervention) 20.0%, a₁ (SMS) 17.3%, a₃ (recall) 10.4%. The policy recommended a change from the behaviour action in 83.9% of child-step decisions. Off-policy evaluation using weighted importance sampling estimated a policy improvement of 6.1%, and fitted Q-evaluation estimated 6.7% (Table 4). The concordance between the two methods, which rely on different assumptions, strengthens confidence in the improvement estimate.

## Community-level bandit allocation

The LinUCB contextual bandit, operating across 1,140 communities with 19-dimensional context, converged predominantly to SMS allocation (99.6% of communities; Supplementary Figure S9). This reflects SMS dominance in community-level cost-effectiveness: at ₦50 per child versus ₦500–₦1,500 for alternatives, SMS provides the highest return per unit cost when applied within communities. Budget sensitivity analysis showed identical allocation patterns across ₦250 million, ₦500 million, and ₦1 billion budgets, as the SMS-dominated allocation cost only ₦184,550 nationally. The constraint became binding only for CHW-level interventions, which would cost approximately ₦1.71 billion.

## Microsimulation: DTP3 completion, cost-effectiveness, and equity

All five intervention scenarios improved DTP3 completion relative to the status quo (Table 5; Figure 8). Under S0 (status quo), DTP3 completion was 85.9% (95% CI: 85.2–86.6%) at ₦155 per child. Two scenarios were dominant — achieving better outcomes at lower cost: uniform SMS (S1; DTP3 87.1%, 95% CI: 86.4–87.7%; ₦98/child; ICER −₦4,591) and bandit-allocated (S5; DTP3 87.1%, 95% CI: 86.5–87.8%; ₦98/child; ICER −₦4,619).

Among non-dominant strategies, risk-targeted intervention (S3) was the most cost-effective: DTP3 88.2% (95% CI: 87.6–88.8%) at ₦341 per child (ICER ₦8,007). This captured approximately 92% of the DTP3 gain from universal CHW deployment (S2; DTP3 88.4%) at 35% of the cost. The RL-optimised policy (S4) achieved equivalent DTP3 completion to S3 (88.2%, 95% CI: 87.5–88.8%) but at 2.6 times the cost (₦903 vs ₦341; ICER ₦32,434), driven by the CQL policy's preference for higher-cost CHW and incentive interventions.

All scenarios reduced the wealth-related equity gap. Under S0, the gap was 7.8 percentage points (poorest 84.2%, richest 92.0%). The largest reductions were achieved by S2 (gap 6.6 pp) and S3 (6.7 pp). The RL policy (S4; 6.8 pp), SMS (S1; 7.2 pp), and bandit (S5; 7.3 pp) also narrowed the gap. No scenario worsened equity, satisfying the a priori constraint.

## RL-optimised versus static targeting

The RL-optimised policy matched risk-targeted intervention on DTP3 completion but did not outperform it. This equivalence arose because the CQL policy's weighted-average relative risk reduction across its recommended action mix was approximately 13.1%, lower than the 20% achievable with universal CHW, because conservative regularisation assigned 20.0% of decisions to no intervention (a₀, 0% RRR). Three structural factors constrained the RL advantage: a short two-transition horizon that limits the scope for sequential decision-making; a narrow relative risk reduction range across actions (10–25%) that reduces the opportunity for learned policies to exploit action differences; and an inferred behaviour policy derived from observational DHS data that introduces proxy error into trajectory construction. The RL framework's primary contribution in this setting is methodological — demonstrating the feasibility of framing vaccination dropout as a sequential decision problem and applying offline reinforcement learning to DHS data — rather than achieving performance superiority over well-designed static targeting.
