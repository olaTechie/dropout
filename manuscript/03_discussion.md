## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Principal Findings

This study pursued three integrated aims: developing cascade-specific dropout prediction models for the DTP1→DTP2 (T1) and DTP2→DTP3 (T2) transitions in Nigeria, training an offline reinforcement learning (RL) policy for sequential intervention allocation, and embedding the optimised policy in a CHEERS 2022-compliant microsimulation. Three headline findings emerge from Table 2, Table 3, and Figure 3. First, transition-specific XGBoost classifiers discriminated well at both steps after isotonic recalibration — T1 AUROC 0.910 (95% CI 0.885–0.932); T2 AUROC 0.943 (95% CI 0.929–0.957). Second, the RL-optimised scenario (S4) increased simulated DTP3 coverage by 1.07 pp over the status quo at ₦748/child; however, the risk-targeted strategy (S3) achieved a comparable coverage gain at nearly half the cost (₦445/child, ICER ₦301/pp versus ₦699/pp for S4). At the reference willingness-to-pay of ₦50,000/pp, S3 dominated the cost-effectiveness plane with 56.7% probability of being cost-effective while S4 returned 0% — an honest finding that must not be obscured. The RL policy's cost-effectiveness advantage emerges only at willingness-to-pay thresholds above approximately ₦70,000/pp. Third, S4 narrowed the absolute wealth-quintile gap in DTP3 completion by approximately 10% relative to the status quo, though S3 and S5 achieved larger absolute equity reductions.

### 4.2 Comparison with Literature

The discrimination of our cascade-specific models compares favourably with prior immunisation dropout studies. Adedokun et al. [@ref3_adedokun2023zero] documented a DTP1-to-DTP3 dropout rate of 28% in Nigeria's 2018 NDHS — nearly double our 2024 rate of 14.6% — using OLS regression that characterises group-level determinants but does not yield individual risk scores for triage. Tessema et al. [@ref5_tessema2024pentavalent] applied multilevel logistic regression to 358,529 children across 33 sub-Saharan African DHS rounds and identified home delivery (AOR 1.65; 95% CI 1.57–1.73), young maternal age (AOR 1.61; 95% CI 1.51–1.72), and no antenatal care (AOR 1.34; 95% CI 1.23–1.45) as leading determinants — consistent with the dominance of need and predisposing SHAP domains in our T1 model. Wiysonge et al. [@ref43_wiysonge2012individual] and Adegboye et al. [@ref42_adegboye2021factors] similarly identified wealth, education, and healthcare contact as predictors across sub-Saharan Africa but did not distinguish T1 and T2 transitions or apply non-linear machine learning. Laryea et al. [@ref45_laryea2014determinants] observed 71% versus 31–35% DTP3 completion by facility type in Ibadan, consonant with Travel_Times and Nightlights_Composite ranking among the top three T1 SHAP predictors. To our knowledge, this is the first TRIPOD-AI-compliant cascade-specific prediction study in the Nigerian DHS context [@ref17_collins2024tripod; @ref7_adeyinka2025widening; @ref1_uthman2024systematic].

Our cost-effectiveness findings are contextualised against published LMIC ICERs. Eze et al. [@ref9_eze2021mhealth] found mHealth reminders improved childhood immunisation coverage by 5–10 pp at costs below USD 2/child, broadly consistent with our Uniform SMS scenario (ICER ₦137/pp). Jacobson Vann et al. [@ref49_cochrane2018reminder] confirmed in a Cochrane meta-analysis that patient reminders consistently increase immunisation uptake across settings. Banerjee et al. [@ref11_banerjee2010improving] reported that incentivised immunisation campaigns in rural India raised DTP3 coverage from 6% to 39% at approximately USD 28/fully immunised child — a higher cost consistent with the intensive programme context. Mekonnen et al. [@ref46_mekonnen2021mobile] documented a pooled OR of approximately 1.89 for mobile phone reminders in sub-Saharan Africa, supporting the plausibility of our parameterised SMS effect. Our S3 ICER of ₦301/pp falls well below WHO-CHOICE thresholds for highly cost-effective interventions in Nigeria [@ref38_marseille2015thresholds; @ref4_who2020ia2030], positioning risk-targeted outreach as an immediately actionable strategy.

### 4.3 Methodological Strengths

Prediction modelling followed TRIPOD-AI standards [@ref17_collins2024tripod], with isotonic recalibration correcting the T2 model's pre-recalibration over-dispersion (slope 1.596 → 0.969) — a step frequently omitted in health prediction literature. The offline RL formulation followed the seven-dimension framework of Gottesman et al. (2019) [@ref30_gottesman2019guidelines], with FQE and WIS off-policy evaluation across three algorithms and zero out-of-distribution actions under all algorithms. Cost-effectiveness analysis adhered to CHEERS 2022 [@ref36_husereau2022consolidated] with 1,000-iteration PSA and equity analysis by wealth quintile. Andersen's behavioural model [@ref8_andersen1995revisiting] framed SHAP domain decomposition, linking model outputs to programme-interpretable domains.

### 4.4 Limitations

Four limitations warrant careful attention. First, vaccination card absence (h1a) was substantially higher among dropout children (37.3% versus 15.2%), introducing possible under-ascertainment of timely vaccination and some misclassification of true completers as dropouts. Second, because DHS records no interventions, behaviour-policy inference assigned proxy actions from dose-timing and health-contact patterns. Although this follows offline RL precedent [@ref30_gottesman2019guidelines; @ref14_komorowski2018artificial], residual confounding is unavoidable: self-motivated attendance and unrecorded programme contacts are both plausible alternative explanations for on-time doses. Third, inherent offline-RL caveats apply — distributional shift between the DHS behaviour policy and any prospective deployment policy cannot be fully eliminated by conservatism regularisation, and partial observability (no supply-side state features) is acknowledged throughout. The completed Gottesman et al. (2019) issue-response checklist in Supplementary §S0.3 documents responses to all seven identified limitations [@ref30_gottesman2019guidelines]. Fourth, the bandit's near-degenerate SMS allocation (99.9%) under the ₦500 million national envelope reflects a budget-constrained choice rather than a clinical recommendation; external validity of the full pipeline beyond Nigeria's 2024 DHS is undemonstrated and deferred to future multi-country validation.

### 4.5 Policy Implications

For NPHCDA and the National Immunisation Programme, the findings suggest a practical two-layer triage protocol consistent with the National Strategic Immunisation Plan [@ref21_nphcda2018nsipss] and Immunisation Agenda 2030 [@ref4_who2020ia2030]. At layer one, the T1 and T2 XGBoost risk scores — exportable as a lookup table requiring no real-time computing infrastructure — stratify children into dropout risk tiers at dose administration. At layer two, the risk-targeted strategy (S3) is the immediately cost-effective action: broad-base SMS reminders for all children, with CHW escalation reserved for the top-decile risk group. This rule achieved ICER ₦301/pp and narrowed the concentration index from 0.0190 to 0.0156, consistent with WHO digital health recommendations [@ref41_who2019digital].

### 4.6 Future Work

A stepped-wedge pragmatic trial of SMS combined with risk-triggered CHW escalation would provide experimental effect-size evidence to replace meta-analytic priors. Uplift modelling would allow more precise targeting of high-benefit children within risk strata [@ref39_chakraborty2013dtr; @ref40_tsiatis2020dtr]. Generalisation to other vaccine antigens (measles, pentavalent antigens) and other LMIC DHS rounds would test external validity, and supply-side state features (cold-chain alerts, stock-outs) could be incorporated as the Nigerian immunisation data infrastructure matures [@ref47_sato2024offline; @ref53_yechezkel2023ppo].
