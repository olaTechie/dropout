# Abstract and Results — Draft

**Study:** Optimising the timing and targeting of interventions to reduce DTP vaccination dropout using offline reinforcement learning: a four-stage analytical framework applied to the Nigeria DHS 2024  
**Journal:** PLOS Digital Health  
**Date:** April 2026

---

## Abstract

### Background

Nigeria accounts for the world's largest absolute burden of under-vaccinated children, with persistent dropout between DTP1 and DTP3 remaining a critical barrier to full immunisation despite improving coverage. Conventional dropout interventions are static — allocated by geography or wealth quintile — and do not adapt to the child's evolving vaccination trajectory. We applied a four-stage analytical framework (immunisation cascade characterisation, machine-learning prediction, offline reinforcement learning, and microsimulation) to identify which children to target, with which intervention, and at which dose transition, using nationally representative survey data.

### Methods and Findings

We analysed 3,194 children aged 12–23 months who had received at least one DTP dose (DTP1), drawn from the Nigeria Demographic and Health Survey 2024. The overall DTP1-to-DTP3 dropout rate was 14.8%, with marked geographic heterogeneity: WHO dropout ranged from 8.6% in South South to 19.6% in North Central geopolitical zone. Transition-specific XGBoost models — trained with 200-trial Bayesian hyperparameter optimisation and cluster-robust five-fold cross-validation — discriminated DTP1→DTP2 dropout with AUC-ROC 0.910 (95% CI 0.885–0.932) and DTP2→DTP3 dropout with AUC-ROC 0.943 (95% CI 0.929–0.957), significantly exceeding logistic regression at both transitions (DeLong p < 1×10⁻⁹). Contrary to the a priori hypothesis that static sociodemographic factors would dominate early-stage dropout, SHAP decomposition showed that dynamic and temporal features — inter-dose intervals, delay accumulation, and community dropout rates — accounted for 35.9% and 59.0% of total mean absolute feature importance at transitions T1 and T2, respectively. A Conservative Q-Learning (CQL; α = 5.0) offline RL policy was trained on inferred DHS trajectories and recommended a diversified intervention mix (CHW home visit 29.4%, conditional incentive 22.9%, SMS reminder 17.3%) compared to the SMS-dominated behaviour policy (70.6%), yielding an estimated 6.1–6.7% improvement in policy value over the status quo. In a 10,000-child microsimulation with 1,000 bootstrap iterations, uniform SMS was a dominant strategy (87.1% DTP3, cost ₦98/child vs ₦155 for status quo at 85.9% DTP3), and risk-targeted allocation was the most cost-effective non-dominant strategy (88.2% DTP3, ICER ₦8,007/full-immunisation course completed). The RL-optimised scenario achieved the same DTP3 rate as risk targeting (88.2%) but at substantially higher cost per child (₦903 vs ₦341; ICER ₦32,434). All six scenarios narrowed the poorest-to-richest DTP3 equity gap from 7.8 to 6.6–7.3 percentage points.

### Conclusions

Dynamic vaccination trajectory features — not static demographics — are the strongest predictors of dropout at both dose transitions, supporting trajectory-responsive intervention design. Risk-targeted allocation guided by machine-learning dropout scores is the most cost-efficient strategy for Nigeria. Offline RL provides a novel framework for framing vaccination dropout as a sequential decision problem and identifies higher-intensity interventions as underutilised; however, it did not outperform well-designed static targeting over a two-transition horizon, primarily due to high out-of-distribution action rates arising from the absence of observed intervention data in the DHS. Applications to longer vaccine schedules with prospective trial data may better demonstrate the sequential advantage of RL.

## Results

### Study population and immunisation cascade

The analytic sample comprised 3,194 children aged 12–23 months who had received DTP1, drawn from the 2024 Nigeria Demographic and Health Survey (NDHS 2024). Eligibility criteria required the child to be alive at the time of interview (b5 = 1), to be the youngest child of their mother to avoid within-cluster dependency, and to have a confirmed DTP1 vaccination record (h3 ∈ {1, 2, 3}). Sample weights, stratification (v022), and primary sampling units (v021) were applied throughout to account for the complex survey design.

The overall DTP1-to-DTP3 dropout rate was 14.8%. Transition-level analysis identified two discrete attrition steps: T1 dropout (DTP1→DTP2 non-completion) occurred in 4.0% of DTP1 recipients (n ≈ 128), and T2 dropout (DTP2→DTP3 non-completion) occurred in 3.9% of DTP2 recipients (n ≈ 119), based on samples of 3,194 and 3,023 children respectively. The T2 model therefore reflects a slightly smaller cohort, with 171 children excluded as DTP2 non-recipients prior to that transition.

Zonal cascade analysis revealed substantial geographic heterogeneity in DTP3 attainment (Table 1; Figure 2). North Central zone had the highest WHO dropout rate (19.6%; DTP3 rate 80.4% of DTP1 recipients), followed by North East (16.8%; DTP3 83.2%) and North West (13.8%; DTP3 86.2%). Southern zones performed markedly better: South South had the lowest dropout rate (8.6%; DTP3 91.4%), followed by South West (13.0%; DTP3 87.0%) and South East (14.1%; DTP3 85.9%). Within-zone T2 retention — defined as the proportion of DTP2 recipients who completed DTP3 — was lowest in North Central (86.9%) and highest in South South (94.5%), indicating that late-stage dropout compounds early attrition particularly in northern zones.

### Descriptive characteristics of the study population

Baseline sociodemographic and enabling characteristics of the full analytic sample, stratified by DTP3 completion status, are presented in Table 1. Dropout children differed from completers across multiple Andersen domains, including wealth quintile, maternal education, distance to health facility, and prior healthcare contact. Full distribution plots are provided in Supplementary Figure S1 (ydata-profiling EDA report) and Supplementary Figure S2 (sweetviz comparative profile). Statistically significant between-group differences are reported with survey-weighted p-values in Table 1.

### Spatial variation in dropout

State-level choropleth mapping of WHO DTP dropout rates (Figure 3) confirmed the zonal gradient identified in the cascade analysis, with a contiguous band of high-dropout states spanning the North Central and North East zones. Local Moran's I analysis identified statistically significant spatial clustering in dropout risk, with hot-spot clusters concentrated in the north-central belt and cold-spot clusters in coastal South South states. Funnel plots of state-level dropout against sample size (Supplementary Figure S3) showed several northern states lying outside the 95% control limits, indicating non-random geographic variation beyond sampling error. Community-level dropout rates, constructed as cluster-aggregated DTP3 non-completion proportions, were retained as a dynamic predictor variable in all downstream models.

### Prediction model discrimination

Transition-specific XGBoost models were trained with Optuna-guided Bayesian hyperparameter optimisation (200 trials per model) using a stratified five-fold cross-validation scheme with cluster-level blocking to prevent information leakage. Both models substantially outperformed logistic regression. The T1 model (DTP1→DTP2 dropout; n = 3,194; event prevalence 4.0%) achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.910 (95% CI 0.885–0.932) versus an AUC-ROC of 0.818 for logistic regression (DeLong difference 0.091, z = 6.14, p = 8.1 × 10⁻¹⁰). The T2 model (DTP2→DTP3 dropout; n = 3,023; event prevalence 3.9%) achieved an AUC-ROC of 0.943 (95% CI 0.929–0.957) versus 0.863 for logistic regression (DeLong difference 0.086, z = 6.11, p = 9.7 × 10⁻¹⁰). Given the low event prevalence at both transitions (approximately 4%), the area under the precision-recall curve (AUC-PR) was also reported: T1 AUC-PR 0.299 (95% CI 0.230–0.387) and T2 AUC-PR 0.548 (95% CI 0.454–0.640). Cross-validated fold AUC-ROC values were stable across folds for both models (T1 range 0.885–0.935; T2 range 0.924–0.969), confirming the absence of overfitting (Table 2; Figure 4).

### Prediction model calibration

Raw XGBoost predictions exhibited miscalibration requiring correction prior to downstream use. The T1 model showed modest under-dispersion (calibration slope 0.874 pre-recalibration; Brier score 0.036), while the T2 model showed over-confident high-risk predictions (calibration slope 1.596 pre-recalibration; Brier score 0.027). Isotonic regression recalibration, fit on out-of-fold predictions from the cluster-robust five-fold cross-validation, corrected both models substantially: T1 recalibrated slope 0.959 (Brier 0.032; reliability term ≈ 0), T2 recalibrated slope 0.969 (Brier 0.026; reliability term ≈ 0). Calibration plots before and after recalibration are shown in Figure 4; recalibrated model outputs were used for all downstream RL trajectory construction and microsimulation inputs.

### SHAP feature importance and Andersen domain decomposition

To characterise which domains of the Andersen Behavioural Model drove dropout prediction, SHAP values were aggregated by domain and reported as mean absolute SHAP across all children and cross-validation folds (Table 3; Figure 5). Contrary to the a priori hypothesis — which predicted predisposing sociodemographic factors to dominate T1 dropout and enabling/need factors to dominate T2 dropout — dynamic and temporal features were the dominant domain at both transitions and in the full dropout model.

In the T1 model, the mean |SHAP| by domain was: Dynamic 1.635, Need 1.237, Enabling 0.897, and Predisposing 0.788. Dynamic features thus accounted for 35.9% of total mean absolute SHAP importance (1.635/4.557). In the T2 model, the dominance of dynamic features was even more pronounced: Dynamic 1.240, Need 0.414, Enabling 0.235, Predisposing 0.212, corresponding to 59.0% of total importance (1.240/2.101). In the full dropout model (DTP1-to-DTP3 non-completion), dynamic features accounted for 52.1% of total importance (2.739/5.256). The leading dynamic predictors across models were inter-dose interval (time elapsed between DTP doses), cumulative delay accumulation, community-level dropout rate, and cluster-level DTP coverage — features reflecting the child's vaccination trajectory rather than their fixed sociodemographic profile. This finding has a direct implication for intervention targeting: the strongest policy signals are not who a child is, but how their vaccination history has unfolded.

### Offline reinforcement learning

The MDP was constructed from 6,217 trajectory rows covering 3,138 children across up to two dose-step transitions. The state space comprised 55 dimensions spanning all four Andersen domains plus dynamic features. The action space comprised five options: a₀ no intervention, a₁ SMS reminder (₦50/child), a₂ CHW home visit (₦500), a₃ facility recall and defaulter tracing (₦1,500), and a₄ conditional cash incentive (₦800). Because the DHS does not record actual interventions delivered, the behaviour policy was inferred from observed vaccination patterns using proxy rules: timely next-dose receipt, fieldworker visit records, vaccination card presence, and campaign vaccination flags. Under this inference, the behaviour policy was strongly SMS-dominated: a₁ (SMS) 70.6%, a₀ (no intervention) 20.9%, a₂ (CHW) 8.3%, a₃ (recall) 0.2%, a₄ (incentive) 0.0%.

FQI (Fitted Q-Iteration) with Extra-Trees base learners (200 estimators) converged at iteration 2 (ΔQ = 0.000). CQL regularisation was applied with α ∈ {1.0, 2.0, 5.0}; the target of OOD frequency below 15% was not achievable at any tested α because actions a₃ and a₄ were essentially absent from the behaviour data (0.2% and 0.0% respectively), making any policy recommending these actions technically out-of-distribution by construction. The most conservative setting, α = 5.0, was selected, yielding OOD frequency 33.3%. The learned CQL policy substantially diversified intervention recommendations relative to the behaviour policy: a₂ (CHW) 29.4%, a₄ (incentive) 22.9%, a₀ (no intervention) 20.0%, a₁ (SMS) 17.3%, and a₃ (recall) 10.4% (Figure 6; Table 4). The policy recommended a change from the behaviour action in 83.9% of child-step decisions.

Off-policy evaluation using Weighted Importance Sampling (WIS) estimated a policy value of 1.171 versus the behaviour baseline of 1.104 — an improvement of 6.1%. Fitted Q-Evaluation (FQE), which provided a model-based cross-check, estimated the CQL policy value at 1.113 versus a behaviour value of 1.043, a 6.7% improvement. Both OPE methods were consistent in direction and magnitude, supporting the robustness of the estimated improvement. The 33.3% OOD rate represents a meaningful caveat: the CQL policy's recommendations for a₃ and a₄ extrapolate substantially beyond the training distribution, and their estimated benefits carry higher uncertainty than a₁ and a₂ recommendations.

### Community-level bandit allocation

A LinUCB contextual bandit was fit across 1,140 communities, using a 19-dimensional context vector comprising sociodemographic and geospatial covariates. Under a ₦500 million budget constraint (scaled to realistic population sizes of approximately 3,000 children per LGA), the bandit converged predominantly towards SMS allocation. This reflects the favourable benefit-to-cost profile of SMS for communities with moderate dropout rates (below approximately 10%): the marginal gain from more intensive CHW-based allocation did not justify the ten-fold cost differential in the majority of communities. Universal CHW allocation would require approximately ₦1.71 billion, substantially exceeding the budget constraint. The bandit's community-level allocation therefore constituted a near-uniform SMS recommendation for most communities, functionally equivalent to the S1 scenario in the microsimulation.

### Microsimulation: DTP3 completion rates and cost-effectiveness

The microsimulation was conducted on a synthetic population of 10,000 children with 1,000 bootstrap iterations. Effect sizes were applied as relative risk reductions on the status-quo dropout probability: SMS 10% RRR, CHW 20% RRR, facility recall 25% RRR, and conditional incentive 14% RRR. Six intervention scenarios were evaluated (Table 5; Figure 7).

Under the status quo (S0), the simulated DTP3 completion rate was 85.9% (95% CI 85.2–86.6%) at a cost of ₦155 per child. Two scenarios were dominant — achieving higher DTP3 rates at lower cost than S0. Uniform SMS coverage (S1) yielded a DTP3 rate of 87.1% (95% CI 86.4–87.7%) at ₦98/child (ICER −₦4,591; dominant). The bandit-allocated scenario (S5) produced near-identical results: DTP3 87.1% (95% CI 86.5–87.8%), cost ₦98/child (ICER −₦4,619; dominant), reflecting the bandit's convergence to SMS for most communities.

Among non-dominant strategies, uniform CHW deployment (S2) produced the highest DTP3 rate of 88.4% (95% CI 87.8–89.0%) at ₦979/child (ICER ₦32,742/full-immunisation course). Risk-targeted allocation (S3) — directing high-intensity interventions to children in the top predicted dropout quintile and SMS to the remainder — achieved 88.2% (95% CI 87.6–88.8%) at ₦341/child (ICER ₦8,007), making it the most cost-effective non-dominant strategy: it captured approximately 92% of the DTP3 gain from universal CHW at 35% of the cost. The RL-optimised scenario (S4) also achieved 88.2% (95% CI 87.5–88.8%), identical to S3 within simulation variance, but at ₦903/child (ICER ₦32,434), approaching the cost of universal CHW. The cost-effectiveness frontier is presented in Figure 8.

### Equity analysis

Across all six scenarios, the wealth-related DTP3 equity gap — defined as the difference in DTP3 completion rates between the poorest and richest wealth quintiles — was reduced relative to status quo. Under S0, the equity gap was 7.8 percentage points (pp; 95% CI 5.7–9.8pp), with poorest-quintile DTP3 completion of 84.2% versus 92.0% in the richest quintile. All interventions narrowed this gap: S2 (CHW) reduced it most (to 6.6pp, 95% CI 4.7–8.5pp), followed by S3 risk-targeted (6.7pp, 95% CI 4.9–8.6pp), S4 RL-optimised (6.9pp, 95% CI 5.1–9.0pp), S1 uniform SMS (7.2pp, 95% CI 5.1–9.3pp), and S5 bandit (7.3pp, 95% CI 5.3–9.2pp). No scenario worsened equity, confirming that intervention strategies that prioritise high-risk children — who are disproportionately from poorer households — are equity-compatible.

### Comparison of RL-optimised versus static strategies

The RL-optimised scenario (S4) achieved the same DTP3 outcome as risk-targeted allocation (S3) at approximately 2.6 times the cost per child (₦903 vs ₦341). This equivalence in effectiveness arose because the CQL policy's weighted-average RRR across its recommended action mix was approximately 13.1% — lower than the 20% RRR achievable with universal CHW deployment, because CQL conservatism assigned 20.0% of decisions to the null intervention (a₀, RRR = 0%). The sequential advantage that RL theoretically provides over static targeting — adapting to dose-step context — was constrained in this application by three structural factors: a short two-transition horizon that limits the scope for sequential decision-making to show differentiated benefit; a narrow RRR range across actions (10–25%) that reduces the opportunity for learned policies to exploit action differences; and an inferred behaviour policy derived from observational DHS data that introduces proxy error into the trajectory construction. These limitations are acknowledged as key constraints on the present findings and are discussed in relation to future applications with longer vaccine schedules or prospectively collected intervention data.
