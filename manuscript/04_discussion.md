# Discussion

## Principal findings

This study developed and evaluated the first application of offline reinforcement learning to vaccination dropout, using a four-stage analytical framework applied to the Nigeria Demographic and Health Survey 2024. Among 3,194 children aged 12-23 months who received DTP1, the overall dropout rate was 14.8%, with transition-specific rates of 4.0% (DTP1→DTP2) and 3.9% (DTP2→DTP3). Three principal findings emerged. First, transition-specific XGBoost models achieved strong discrimination (AUC-ROC 0.910-0.943), and SHAP decomposition by Andersen's Behavioural Model revealed that dynamic temporal features, particularly inter-dose intervals, delay accumulation, and community dropout rates, dominated prediction at both transitions (36-59% of SHAP importance), challenging the hypothesis of distinct predisposing versus enabling/need predictor profiles. Second, the CQL-learned sequential policy achieved a 6-7% improvement over the inferred behaviour policy on off-policy evaluation, but did not clearly outperform well-designed static targeting in microsimulation. Third, risk-targeted intervention, directing community health worker visits to the highest-risk 30% of children with SMS reminders for the remainder, was the most cost-effective non-dominant strategy (ICER ₦8,007 per additional DTP3 completion), while all intervention scenarios reduced the equity gap between the poorest and richest quintiles.

## Comparison with previous studies

### Dropout prevalence and determinants

The 14.8% dropout rate in our DTP1-restricted sample is consistent with previous estimates from Nigeria. Adedokun and colleagues reported a national DTP1-to-DTP3 dropout rate of 28% using the 2018 NDHS [3], while the 2024 survey data suggest improvement, consistent with the declining trend from 46% (2003) to 28% (2018) reported across four NDHS waves [6]. The geographical heterogeneity we observed, with the North Central zone showing the highest dropout (19.6%) and South South the lowest (8.6%), aligns with the widening zonal disparities documented by Adeyinka and colleagues [7]. Our restriction to DTP1 recipients yields a lower headline dropout rate than population-level estimates that include zero-dose children, an important distinction when interpreting programme performance [2].

The dominance of dynamic temporal features in our SHAP analysis contrasts with the static determinant profiles typically reported in dropout studies. Tessema and colleagues, in a multilevel analysis of 358,529 children across 33 sub-Saharan African countries, identified maternal age, home delivery, lack of antenatal care, and low education as key dropout determinants [5], all of which fall within the predisposing and enabling domains. Uthman and colleagues similarly found wealth, maternal education, and antenatal care attendance as dominant predictors of vaccination coverage in Nigeria [1]. Our finding that inter-dose intervals and community dropout rates outweigh these conventional determinants suggests that the temporal dynamics of the vaccination cascade carry predictive information that static cross-sectional analyses cannot capture. This is consistent with the broader insight that dropout is a sequential process: the strongest signal for whether a child will complete the series comes from how their cascade has progressed thus far, not from baseline demographic characteristics.

### Reinforcement learning in health

Our application of offline RL to vaccination extends a growing literature on RL for clinical decision-making. Komorowski and colleagues demonstrated the potential of fitted Q-iteration for sepsis treatment optimisation using the MIMIC-III database, reporting that the AI Clinician policy was associated with lower mortality than clinician policies [35]. Subsequent work has applied CQL to mechanical ventilation management [36] and loop diuretic dosing [37], establishing CQL as the preferred offline RL algorithm for healthcare applications due to its conservative value estimation that mitigates overestimation of out-of-distribution actions [31]. Our study extends this methodological family to a novel domain: childhood immunisation retention. The two-stage WIS-then-FQE evaluation strategy we employed follows the recommendations of Raghu and colleagues, who demonstrated that FQE provides the best ranking quality for policy selection in healthcare settings [32].

A key distinction of our application is that the behaviour policy is inferred rather than observed. In the sepsis literature, clinician actions are directly recorded in electronic health records. In our setting, the DHS does not record which interventions were deployed to each child, requiring inference from observed timeliness, fieldworker visits, and vaccination venue. This proxy inference introduces additional uncertainty that may explain why our RL policy improvement (6-7%) is modest compared with the improvements reported in sepsis studies. Future applications using electronic immunisation registries, which record actual reminder and follow-up activities, could substantially reduce this limitation.

### Microsimulation and cost-effectiveness

The finding that risk-targeted intervention (S3) is the most cost-effective non-dominant strategy aligns with broader evidence on targeting efficiency in immunisation programmes. Our ICER of ₦8,007 per additional DTP3 completion (approximately USD 5.3 at 2024 exchange rates) is well below conventional cost-effectiveness thresholds for health interventions in Nigeria [38]. The dominance of SMS-based strategies (S1, S5), which achieved better outcomes at lower cost than the status quo, is consistent with the meta-analytic evidence from Eze and colleagues showing that mobile health reminders significantly improve vaccination coverage, with stronger effects in low-income settings (RR 1.39, 95% CI 1.19-1.62) [9].

That the RL-optimised policy (S4) did not clearly outperform risk-targeted intervention (S3) warrants careful interpretation. In the dynamic treatment regime literature, the advantage of sequential optimisation over static targeting grows with the number of decision points and the heterogeneity of treatment effects across time [39,40]. Our two-transition horizon (DTP1→DTP2→DTP3) may be too short for sequential advantage to manifest. The five-dose pentavalent series or the full expanded programme on immunisation schedule, with more transitions and greater scope for temporal differentiation, may provide a more suitable setting for RL-based optimisation. Additionally, the narrow range of relative risk reductions across our five actions (10-25%) limits the potential for RL to exploit differential treatment effects.

## Policy implications

Three findings carry direct policy implications for immunisation programme managers in Nigeria. First, the dominance of SMS as a cost-effective floor intervention supports the ongoing scale-up of mobile health platforms for vaccination reminders, consistent with WHO recommendations [41]. Universal SMS reminders reduced dropout from 14.1% to 12.9% in our microsimulation at a cost saving relative to the status quo, representing approximately 120,000 additional DTP3 completions nationally per year.

Second, risk-targeted deployment of community health worker visits to the highest-risk 30% of children, identified using the prediction model, offers the best return on investment when budgets exceed the SMS-only threshold. This strategy achieved 88.2% DTP3 completion at one-third the cost of universal CHW deployment and should be considered for programmes that can implement risk stratification at the facility level.

Third, the finding that dynamic temporal features, rather than static demographics, are the strongest predictors of dropout has implications for intervention timing. Programmes should monitor inter-dose intervals and delay accumulation in real time and trigger escalated interventions (from SMS to CHW visit) when a child's cascade trajectory indicates increasing risk, rather than relying solely on baseline risk profiles determined at the first visit.

## Strengths and limitations

This study has several strengths. It is the first to apply offline reinforcement learning to vaccination dropout, demonstrating the feasibility of framing immunisation retention as a sequential decision problem. The use of NDHS 2024, a nationally representative survey with vaccination card dates, enabled reconstruction of temporal trajectories. The four-stage framework, integrating machine learning, reinforcement learning, multi-armed bandits, and microsimulation, provides a comprehensive analytical toolkit for intervention optimisation. Model validation was rigorous, incorporating cluster-robust cross-validation, isotonic recalibration, and multiple off-policy evaluation methods. All intervention scenarios were assessed for equity impact, and the code is publicly available for replication.

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the DHS is cross-sectional, and vaccination trajectories were reconstructed retrospectively from card records rather than observed longitudinally. Card-confirmed dates were available for approximately 70% of the sample; children with mother-reported or card-without-date vaccination status lacked temporal information. Second, the behaviour policy was inferred from observed patterns rather than recorded, introducing uncertainty into the RL training data. The 33.3% out-of-distribution action frequency in the CQL policy reflects this limitation: the algorithm is recommending interventions (facility recall, incentives) that were essentially unobserved in the data, relying on extrapolation rather than evidence. Third, the intervention effect sizes used in the microsimulation were derived from published trials in various settings, not from Nigeria-specific randomised evidence. The effect sizes were applied as relative risk reductions, which avoids ceiling effects but may still over- or under-estimate the true impact in the Nigerian context. Fourth, the low event rates for transition-specific dropout (4.0% and 3.9%) mean that the prediction models were trained on sparse positive examples, and the AUC-PR values (0.299 and 0.548), while reasonable for such imbalanced outcomes, indicate limited precision in identifying individual children who will drop out. Fifth, the two-transition decision horizon limits the potential for sequential advantage over static policies. Sixth, the community-level variables in the dynamic domain (community dropout rate, cluster DTP3 coverage) may capture unobserved confounding rather than causal peer effects, and their dominance in the SHAP analysis should be interpreted with this caveat.

## Conclusion

This study demonstrates the first application of offline reinforcement learning to vaccination dropout, providing a methodological framework for sequential intervention optimisation in immunisation programmes. While the RL-optimised policy achieved modest improvement over the status quo and did not clearly outperform well-designed static targeting in the two-transition DTP setting, the framework identifies risk-targeted deployment of community health worker visits as the most cost-effective strategy for reducing dropout in Nigeria. The finding that dynamic temporal features dominate dropout prediction challenges conventional static determinant analyses and suggests that real-time monitoring of vaccination cascade trajectories could improve intervention targeting. Future work should apply this framework to longer vaccination schedules, utilise electronic immunisation registries with recorded intervention data, and evaluate the approach in prospective implementation studies.
